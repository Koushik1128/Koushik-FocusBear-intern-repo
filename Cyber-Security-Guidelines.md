# Cyber Security Guidelines

## Research & Learn

**Common cyber security threats in a remote work environment**

Working remotely widens the attack surface compared to an office network. The most common threats are phishing emails and messages designed to steal credentials, unsecured home or public Wi-Fi that can be intercepted, weak or reused passwords, outdated software with unpatched vulnerabilities, and personal devices being used for work without proper security controls. Shared or public computers, and leaving a laptop unlocked in a cafe or coworking space, are also common ways data gets exposed outside a controlled office environment.

**Best practices for keeping devices and accounts secure**

Keep the operating system and applications updated so known vulnerabilities get patched. Use unique, strong passwords for every account, ideally generated and stored in a password manager rather than reused or memorized. Enable two factor authentication wherever it is offered. Use a firewall and reputable antivirus or endpoint protection. Avoid installing software from untrusted sources, and be cautious on public Wi Fi, using a VPN if handling company data on an untrusted network.

**Why locking your computer when away matters**

An unlocked computer is an open door. Anyone walking past, whether a stranger in a cafe or someone in a shared household or office, could access emails, company systems, or files in seconds. Locking the screen whenever stepping away, even briefly, removes that window of opportunity entirely, and is one of the simplest habits with the biggest security payoff for the least effort.

**Handling phishing attempts and suspicious links**

Phishing emails typically create urgency, impersonate a trusted sender, or ask you to click a link or open an attachment unexpectedly. Before clicking, check the sender's actual email address rather than just the display name, hover over links to see the real destination URL, and be suspicious of any message pushing you to act immediately or bypass normal process. If in doubt, do not click, verify through a separate channel such as messaging the sender directly, and report it to IT or the relevant team rather than deleting it silently, since others may have received the same message.

**What makes a strong password, and why use a password manager**

A strong password is long (at least 12 to 16 characters), random rather than a predictable phrase, and unique to that one account so a breach on one site cannot be used to break into others. Humans are bad at remembering many long random strings, which is exactly why people fall back on short, reused, or pattern based passwords. A password manager solves this by generating and storing strong unique passwords for every account, so the only thing you need to remember is one master password.

**Why two factor authentication (2FA) matters**

2FA adds a second proof of identity beyond just a password, typically something you have (a phone, an authenticator app, a hardware key) rather than something you know. Even if a password is stolen or leaked in a breach, 2FA stops an attacker from actually logging in without that second factor. It should be enabled on anything holding sensitive data or access, especially email (since email is often the recovery path into everything else), work accounts, financial accounts, and code repositories.

## Reflection

**What security measures do I currently follow, and where can I improve?**

I already have 2FA enabled on most of my main accounts, and my laptop and phone are set to auto lock after a short idle period, so those two habits were already solid. The gap was passwords: I had been memorizing and reusing passwords across accounts instead of using a password manager. I have now set up a password manager and migrated my accounts over to unique generated passwords, so that gap is closed.

**How can I make secure behaviour a habit rather than an afterthought?**

Tying it to something I already do daily works better than a one off effort. For example, treating "log into a new account" as always meaning "generate it through the password manager" rather than typing something familiar, so the secure path becomes the default path instead of an extra step to remember.

**What steps will I take to ensure my passwords and accounts are secure?**

I set up a password manager and migrated my most important accounts (email, GitHub, work tools) to unique generated passwords. Going forward, any new account gets a generated password from the manager rather than a memorized one.

**What would I do if I suspected a security breach or suspicious activity on my account?**

Change the password immediately on the affected account and any account that reused the same or a similar password, check whether 2FA is still correctly configured (in case it was disabled by the attacker), review recent login activity or sessions and revoke anything unrecognized, and report it to Focus Bear's relevant contact or IT so the team is aware and can check for wider impact.
